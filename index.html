    Introduction
    
								
    
                                
    In 1769, James Watt improved the steam engine, which brought a new source of power to human beings, as well as making a dramatically change in people's lives. Since the great invention was equipped with gears in diverse shape and function, it ushered in the age of machine and triggered the first industrial revolution.
 
								During the 1870s, the invention of alternating current fostering the rapid growth of industries in most developed countries. It is known as the second industrial revolution that machines were driven by stable and efficient source of energy and became more widely used than before.
 
								After the World War II, the rapid development and popularization of electronics and Internet technology started off the third industrial revolution-the digital revolution. Quantum theory, aerospace technology, and genetic engineering all quickly took off, altering nearly every industry.
 
								The first and second industrial revolutions belong to the engineer; the third industrial revolution belongs to the programmer. We know each of these first three industrial revolutions represented profound change, and now we are at the beginning of the forth Industrial Revolution. It will belong to the scientist.
 
								The improvement of machine and the discovery of new source of power lead to the advancement of technology. Nano-opto-machine (NOM), a light driven gear device, will bring a completely new vision to the science world. In the past, scientists have attempted to use electric or magnetic fields as a source of energy at the nanoscale, but this time, we aim to utilize the light as a new source of energy with the benefit of accurate and precise to our subject. Our strategy is to cast light on the asymmetric DNA origami made gear causing it to spin. In succeeding (If it works out,) the outcome will provide an efficient way to design and control the nanomachine!

    Structure
    
								
    
                            
                            
    Our standard synthesis protocol involves a 50ul reaction volume with 20nM m13 plasmid, 200nM of the appropriate staples, 20mM MgCl, 1mM EDTA and 5mM Tris buffer. The samples are annealed using the following temperature cycle:
    

							
      
							  
    • Denature at 65C for 15 minutes
      • 
							  
    • Slow ramp to 45C at 0.1C per 7 minutes
      • 
							  
    • Hold at 20C
      • 
							
    

							
    To purify the samples they are mixed with PEG precipitation buffer (15% w/v PEG 8000, 5mM Tris, 1mM EDTA, 505mM NaCl) in 1:1 ratio. The mixture is then spun hard for 25 minutes at room temperature and the supernatant is discarded. The DNA origami pellet is then left to resuspend at room temperature for 24 hours at 20nM in DNA origami buffer 20mM MgCl, 1mM EDTA and 5mM Tris.)
